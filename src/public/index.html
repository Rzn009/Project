    About Quantum

    Quantum is a domain in physics that studies the behavior of very small particles, such as atoms and subatomic particles. This theory describes the properties of these particles in terms of probability, where a particle has no definite state until it is measured or observed. Unique phenomena such as superposition and entanglement are characteristic of quantum physics.

    The physicist

    James Clerk Maxwell

    was a Scottish physicist who made significant contributions to the field of theoretical physics, particularly in the study of electromagnetism. Born on June 13, 1831, in Edinburgh, Scotland, Maxwell demonstrated exceptional intellectual abilities from a young age. He came from a well-educated family and showed a keen interest in mathematics and physics.

    Albert Einstein

    was a German-born physicist who made groundbreaking contributions to the field of theoretical physics, reshaping our understanding of the universe. Born on March 14, 1879, in Ulm, in the Kingdom of Württemberg in the German Empire, Einstein displayed extraordinary intellectual prowess from an early age. Hailing from a middle-class Jewish family, he demonstrated a profound interest in mathematics and physics.

    Isaac Newton

    was an English physicist and mathematician who made profound contributions to the field of theoretical physics, particularly in the study of classical mechanics and the law of universal gravitation. Born on December 25, 1642, in Woolsthorpe, Lincolnshire, England, Newton exhibited exceptional intellectual abilities and curiosity from an early age. Despite facing personal challenges and financial difficulties, he pursued his passion for learning and exploration.
